In the last lecture we have seen that we can typically treat complex atomic systems within the central field approximation.

So we we can can treat atoms through the shell structure known from the H\mathrm{H}H atom, but the screening lifts the lll degeneracy. For a single outer electron, we have even seen how this screening can be described by the quantum defect.

We would now like to go beyond this simple picture and discuss the following questions:

  • How should the residual term H^1\hat{H}_1H^1​ be taken into account?

  • How do we properly take into account the Pauli principle ?

  • How can we treat the fine-splitting ?

On the residual coupling

If we ignore the residual coupling, we obtain a spherically symmetric problem, which implies that the angular momentum l⃗i\vec{l}_ili​ of each electron is conserved. This conservation will be broken by H^1\hat{H}_{1}H^1​. However, these forces are internal, which implies that the total angular momentum L⃗=∑il⃗i\vec{L} = \sum_i \vec{l}_iL=∑i​li​ is conserved. So we should label the states in the complex Hamiltonian by L⃗\vec{L}L.

The total angular momentum will then set the symmetry of the spatial wavefunction. As already discussed in some detail for the He atom, this has wide-reaching consquence on the spin degree of freedom through exchange interaction.

The Pauli principle and spin

  • According to the Pauli principle, each single-particle state can be occupied only by one electron. After distributing all electrons over different single-particle eigenstates ("orbitals"), the resulting state needs to be fully antisymmetrized (Slater determinant).

  • There is a simplification for atoms with many electrons: The angular momenta and spins of a complete subshell with n, l, {m−l,⋯ ,ml}n,\,l,\,\{m_{-l},\cdots,m_l\}n,l,{m−l​,⋯,ml​} add to zero and can be ignored in the further considerations ("shell structure"). Note that this is often broken in molecular binding!

  • Alkali atoms are the simplest atoms with shell structure: All but one valence electron add to L=0,S=0L=0,S=0L=0,S=0. The ground state thus has L=0,S=1/2L=0,S=1/2L=0,S=1/2.

  • For more complex atoms, the valence electrons couple to a total orbital angular momentum LLL with a given symmetry according to particle exchange.

  • 1s2→L=0,S=01s^2 \rightarrow L=0,S=01s2→L=0,S=0. The corresponding term is 1S^1S1S

  • 1s2s→L=0,{S=0,S=1}1s2s \rightarrow L=0, \{S=0,S=1\}1s2s→L=0,{S=0,S=1}. The corresponding terms are 1S^1S1S and 3S^3S3S. .

Fine splitting

We have already seen in the discussion of the hydrogen atom that relativistic effects should be taken into account to fully understand the level spectrum of different atoms. To take into account the spin, we can decompose the Hamiltonian as follows:

H^=∑iN(12∇⃗r⃗i2+Vcf(ri))⏟H^0+∑j>iN(1rij−S(ri))⏟H^1+∑iNci(r⃗i)L⃗^i⋅S⃗^i⏟H^2 \hat{H} = \underbrace{\sum_i^N \left( \frac{1}{2} \vec{\nabla}^2_{\vec{r}_i} + V_\textrm{cf} (r_i) \right)}_{\hat{H}_0} + \underbrace{\sum^N_{j>i} \left( \frac{1}{r_{ij}} - S(r_i) \right)}_{\hat{H}_1} + \underbrace{\sum_i^N c_i(\vec{r}_i) \hat{\vec{L}}_i \cdot \hat{\vec{S}}_i}_{\hat{H}_2}H^=H^0​i∑N​(21​∇ri​2​+Vcf​(ri​))​​+H^1​j>i∑N​(rij​1​−S(ri​))​​+H^2​i∑N​ci​(ri​)L^i​⋅S^i​​​

The term H^0\hat{H}_0H^0​ is from the central field and the independent particle model. The Hamiltonian H^1\hat{H}_1H^1​ results from the residual electrostatic interaction and the Hamiltonian H^2\hat{H}_2H^2​ comes from the spin-orbit coupling of individual electrons.

The question is now which term dominates. Since H^2∝(Zα)2\hat{H}_2 \propto (Z\alpha)^2H^2​∝(Zα)2, we can ignore it if ZZZ is small. This is the case for light atoms.

For our example this means that 3P^3P3P is the lowest energy state. However, the triplet state has a multiplicity. Which are these states? There has to be an additional degree of freedom. The total electronic angular momentum J⃗^=L⃗^+S⃗^\hat{\vec{J}} = \hat{\vec{L}} + \hat{\vec{S}}J^=L^+S^ of the atom is a conserved quantity. The basis

∣L,S,Ml,Ms⟩\left|L,S,M_\textrm{l},M_\textrm{s}\right\rangle∣L,S,Ml​,Ms​⟩

is therefore not the right basis, since MlM_\textrm{l}Ml​ and MsM_\textrm{s}Ms​ are not conserved because of LSLSLS coupling. The correct basis is

∣L,S,J,Mj⟩.\left|L,S,J,M_\textrm{j}\right\rangle.∣L,S,J,Mj​⟩.

Which JJJ corresponds to the ground state? If the outermost shell is more than half filled, the maximum JJJ value is the ground state, otherwise, the ground state has the minimum JJJ value. In our example, Si\mathrm{Si}Si: 3P0^3P_03P0​ is the ground state!

So far, we have been concerned with the "Russell-Saunders" coupling, also known as LSLSLS coupling. However, for atoms with large ZZZ, the term H^2\hat{H}_2H^2​ in the Hamiltonian might become large, since it is proportional to (Zα)2(Z \alpha)^2(Zα)2. We can ignore H^1\hat{H}_1H^1​ instead.

According to H^2\hat{H}_2H^2​ the individual electron orbital angular momentum lll pairs with the spin sss of the same electron to form the total electron angular momentum jjj. This will lead us to the concept of jjjjjj coupling. Let us consider carbon first (see 1{reference-type="ref" reference="693186"}). The ground state reads:

C:1s22s22p2.\mathrm{C}: 1s^22s^22p^2.C:1s22s22p2.

Let us consider one excited state:

For heavy atoms we can observe two doublets instead of a singlet and a triplet. Hyperfine splitting

Until now we have investigated the structure of the atom depending on:

  • The orbital angular momentum L⃗\vec{L}L, which defines the shells.

  • The total electronic angular momentum J⃗=L⃗+S⃗\vec{J} = \vec{L} + \vec{S}J=L+S, which defines the fine structure because of the spin-orbit coupling.

Further the nucleus has a spin angular momentum I⃗\vec{I}I, which leads to a magnetic moment:

μ⃗I=gIμNI⃗\vec{\mu}_I= g_I \mu_N \vec{I}μ​I​=gI​μN​I

We have introduced the new constants:

  • gIg_IgI​, which is always in the order of one, but it changes due to the changing structure of the different nuclei.

  • The nuclear magneton μN=eℏ2mp=mempμB\mu_N = \frac{e\hbar}{2m_p}= \frac{m_e}{m_p}\mu_BμN​=2mp​eℏ​=mp​me​​μB​.

From the values of the prefactors, we can immediatly deduce that the hyperfine structure will be roughly three orders of magnitude smaller than the fine structure. As with the spin-orbit coupling this nuclear spin will experience the magnetic field produced by the motion of the electrons B⃗el\vec{B}_{el}Bel​ and we have:

Hhfs=−μ⃗I⋅B⃗el=AhfsI⃗J⃗H_{hfs} = -\vec{\mu}_I \cdot \vec{B}_{el}\\ = A_{hfs}\vec{I}\vec{J}Hhfs​=−μ​I​⋅Bel​=Ahfs​IJ

This couples I⃗\vec{I}I and J⃗\vec{J}J and the full structure is given by total angular momentum:

F⃗=J⃗+I⃗\vec{F} = \vec{J} + \vec{I}F=J+I

Hydrogen

In hydrogen the ground state has no angular momentum and the spin is simply S=1/2S=1/2S=1/2. The nucleus has the same, such that we have I=1/2I=1/2I=1/2 and the groundstate splits into the F=0F=0F=0 and the F=1F=1F=1 doublet. They are separated by an energy difference of 1.421.421.42GHz. This corresponds to a transition wavelength of 21 cm, which is widely used in astronomy.

Cesium clocks

The definition of time is based the hyperfine levels of Cs. Cs has just one stable isotope Cs-133 with nuclear spin I=7/2I=7/2I=7/2. The ground state electron is in the the 6s state in the 2S1/2^2 S_{1/2}2S1/2​ state. So the groundstate splits into the F=3F=3F=3 and F=4F=4F=4 manifold. They are separated by 9.4GHz and the clicking between these transitions is our definition of time. The second is actually defined as:

The second is the duration of 9 192 631 770 periods of the radiation corresponding to the transition between the two hyperfine levels of the ground state of the cesium 133 atom.

All the other units have similiar definitions, except the embarrising kilogram. It is defined as:

The kilogram is the unit of mass; it is equal to the mass of the international prototype of the kilogram.

Bosonic and fermionic isotopes

A particularly interesting problem is the influence of the nuclear spin on how the atoms talk to each other. For example we have two different stable isotope of Li, namely Li 6^66 and Li7^77, which only differ by one neutron in the nucleus. All the wavelengths for controlling the atoms are extremely similiar, however the hyperfine structure is different. For Li6^66 we have a nuclear spin of I=1I=1I=1 and for Li7^77 I=3/2I=3/2I=3/2. The resulting total angular momentum

  • F=1/2F=1/2F=1/2 in the ground state of Li6^66

  • F=1F=1F=1 in the ground state of Li7^77

So one is of them is an integer spin boson and the other one is a spin half fermion. One can nicely see these differences as one tries to put them onto each other for cold temperatures and tight traps as visualized in the Figure below and reproduced from Truscott 2001.
