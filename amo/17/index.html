Lecture 18 - Quantization of the Electromagnetic field

We are looking into the quantization of the electromagnetic field. How to find the conjugate momenta and how we can identify the photon as a quantized particle.

Until now we exclusively treated the atom, molecule etc in a quantum-mechanical way. The electromagnetic field was always treated classically. We will attempt to change that for various reasons:

  • Spontaneous emission and the Lamb shift can only be understood within quantum electrodynamics.

  • Several experiments control the electromagnetic field at the single photon level, so we have to understand how this works.

  • It frankly very unsatifying to only quantize half the problem.

So before we start the endevour let us start out with the some reminders on the properties of radiation in classical electromagnetism.

Maxwell's Equations and Vector Potential

Electrodynamics is described by Maxwell's equations:

∇⃗⋅B⃗=0∇⃗×E⃗+∂B⃗∂t=0∇⃗⋅E⃗=1ϵ0ρ(r⃗,t)∇⃗×B⃗=1c2∂E⃗∂t+1ϵ0c2j⃗\vec{\nabla} \cdot \vec{B} = 0\\ \vec{\nabla} \times \vec{E} + \frac{\partial \vec{B}}{\partial t}=0\\ \vec{\nabla} \cdot \vec{E} = \frac{1}{\epsilon_0}\rho(\vec{r},t) \\ \vec{\nabla} \times \vec{B} = \frac{1}{c^2} \frac{\partial \vec{E}}{\partial t}+\frac{1}{\epsilon_0 c^2}\vec{j}∇⋅B=0∇×E+∂t∂B​=0∇⋅E=ϵ0​1​ρ(r,t)∇×B=c21​∂t∂E​+ϵ0​c21​j​

These equations are the equations of motion for the electromagnetic field. The first two are the homogeneous Maxwell equations and we can directly solve them by setting:

B⃗=∇⃗×A⃗E⃗=−∂∂tA−∇ϕ\vec{B} = \vec{\nabla}\times\vec{A}\\ \vec{E}= -\frac{\partial}{\partial t}A-\nabla \phiB=∇×AE=−∂t∂​A−∇ϕ

Quite importantly, the choice of the potential A⃗\vec{A}A has an inherent gauge freedom as we can basically perform the local gauge transformation:

A⃗→A⃗+∇⃗f(r⃗,t)ϕ→ϕ−∂f∂t\vec{A} \rightarrow \vec{A} + \vec{\nabla} f (\vec{r},t)\\ \phi \rightarrow \phi - \frac{\partial f}{\partial t}A→A+∇f(r,t)ϕ→ϕ−∂t∂f​

So we can transform the electromagnetic field and the magnetic field, but the equations of motion remain the same. It is this local gauge freedom, which also allows us to have charge conservation. But it is also this gauge freedom, which will make the quantization rather technically involved.

Free radiation

To simplify the problem, we will actually, only work on free electromagnetic radiation, which simplifies the remaining Maxwell equations too:

∇⃗⋅E⃗=0∇⃗×B⃗=1c2∂E⃗∂t\vec{\nabla} \cdot \vec{E} = 0 \\ \vec{\nabla} \times \vec{B} = \frac{1}{c^2} \frac{\partial\vec{E}}{\partial t}∇⋅E=0∇×B=c21​∂t∂E​

They become very simple to within the Coulomb gauge, were we fix:

∇⋅A⃗=0 \nabla \cdot \vec{A} = 0∇⋅A=0

For the electric field we have:

∇⃗⋅(−∂∂tA−∇ϕ)=0−Δϕ=0\vec{\nabla} \cdot \left(-\frac{\partial}{\partial t}A-\nabla \phi\right) = 0 \\ -\Delta \phi = 0∇⋅(−∂t∂​A−∇ϕ)=0−Δϕ=0

This is solved through the boring solution ϕ=0\phi = 0ϕ=0. For the magnetic field we obtain1:

∇⃗×(∇×A⃗)=−1c2∂2A⃗∂t2∇2A⃗−1c2∂2A⃗∂t2=0\vec{\nabla} \times (\nabla \times \vec{A}) = -\frac{1}{c^2} \frac{\partial^2\vec{A}}{\partial t^2}\\ \nabla^2 \vec{A} -\frac{1}{c^2} \frac{\partial^2\vec{A}}{\partial t^2}=0∇×(∇×A)=−c21​∂t2∂2A​∇2A−c21​∂t2∂2A​=0

So the free radiation describes a travelling wave in the direction k⃗\vec{k}k. The coulomb gauge further tells us that the vector potential only has components, which are orthogonal to k⃗\vec{k}k as:

Given that these equations of motion for the vector potential look a lot like the ones of a harmonic oscillator, let us go through the quantization of the harmonic oscillator first.

Quantization procedure for the harmonic oscillator

To the the problem, we first have to go back the cooking recipe for the quantization of a classical problem. In a first step, we have to obtain the relevant Lagrangian. Knowing what is it, is a rather interesting artform of theoretical physics, but for most cases you might just take it as a given thing. We have:

LHO=12mx˙2−mω22x2L_{HO} = \frac{1}{2}m\dot{x}^2-\frac{m\omega^2}{2}x^2LHO​=21​mx˙2−2mω2​x2

We can now identify the conjugate momentum as:

p=∂L∂x˙=mx˙p = \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{x}}\\ = m\dot{x}p=∂x˙∂L​=mx˙

At this stage we can calculate the Hamiltonian:

H=x˙p−LHHO=p22m+mω22x2H = \dot{x}p - L\\ H_{HO} = \frac{p^2}{2m}+\frac{m\omega^2}{2}x^2H=x˙p−LHHO​=2mp2​+2mω2​x2

At this stage we can identify the classical equations of motion through:

dxdt=∂H∂pdpdt=−∂H∂x\frac{dx}{dt}= \frac{\partial H}{\partial p}\\ \frac{dp}{dt}= -\frac{\partial H}{\partial x}dtdx​=∂p∂H​dtdp​=−∂x∂H​

It results just in the usual Newtons law. To get now the quantum formulation, we can quantize the system by imposing the commutation relationship on position and its conjugate momentum:

 [x^,p^]=iℏ~[\hat{x}, \hat{p}] = i\hbar [x^,p^​]=iℏ

We then had the final Hamiltonian:

H^=p^22m+mω22x^2\hat{H} = \frac{\hat{p}^2}{2m}+\frac{m\omega^2}{2}\hat{x}^2H^=2mp^​2​+2mω2​x^2

However, we know from the previous lecture that it is much nicer to work in the basis of raising and lowering operators:

a^=12ℏ(mωx+ipmω)a^†=12ℏ(mωx−ipmω) [a^,a^†]=1\hat{a} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\hbar}}\left(\sqrt{m\omega}x+i\frac{p}{\sqrt{m\omega}}\right)\\ \hat{a}^\dag = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\hbar}}\left(\sqrt{m\omega}x-i\frac{p}{\sqrt{m\omega}}\right)\\ ~[\hat{a}, \hat{a}^\dag] =1a^=2ℏ​1​(mω​x+imω​p​)a^†=2ℏ​1​(mω​x−imω​p​) [a^,a^†]=1

for which we obtain:

H^=ℏω(a^†a^+12)\hat{H} =\hbar\omega\left(\hat{a}^\dag \hat{a}+\frac{1}{2}\right)H^=ℏω(a^†a^+21​)

The lagrangien for the electromagnetism

At this stage we would like to roll out the formalism for electromagnetism, following the discussion of Cohen-Tannoudji Vol 3 (appendix of 18). It adds sequentially the following new problems, which we will tackle as we get to them:

  • The Lagrangien has plenty degrees of freedom.

  • The dynamical variables are complex.

We start out with the Lagrangien:

L=∫d3rL(r⃗,t)L(r⃗,t)=ϵ02(E⃗2−c2B⃗2)L = \int d^3r \mathcal{L}(\vec{r},t)\\ \mathcal{L}(\vec{r},t)= \frac{\epsilon_0}{2}\left(\vec{E}^2-c^2\vec{B}^2\right)L=∫d3rL(r,t)L(r,t)=2ϵ0​​(E2−c2B2)

As it depends explicitly on the electric and magnetic field it is manifestly gauge invariant. However, the current version does not allow us to to identify the conjugate variables. We will use the vector potential to introduce them:

L(r⃗,t)=ϵ02(A⃗˙2(r⃗,t)−c2(∇×A⃗)2)\mathcal{L}(\vec{r},t)= \frac{\epsilon_0}{2}\left(\dot{\vec{A}}^2(\vec{r},t)-c^2\left(\nabla \times \vec{A}\right)^2\right)L(r,t)=2ϵ0​​(A˙2(r,t)−c2(∇×A)2)

The conjugate moment and hamiltonian

We have discussed in the section on the quantization of the harmonic oscillator how to find the conjugate momentum for classical variables. Here, we have complex variables. But, we can deduce the conjugate momentum through as decomposition X=x1+ix2X = x_1 + i x_2X=x1​+ix2​. We actually obtain:

P=∂L∂X˙∗P = \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{X}^*}P=∂X˙∗∂L​

Here, it implies that the conjugate momentum Πi(k⃗)\Pi_i(\vec{k})Πi​(k) is

Πi(k⃗)=∂L(k⃗,t)∂A˙i∗(k⃗,t)=ϵ0A˙i(k⃗,t)=−ϵ0Ei(k⃗,t)\Pi_i(\vec{k}) = \frac{\partial\mathcal{L}(\vec{k},t)}{\partial \dot{A}^*_i(\vec{k},t)}\\ = \epsilon_0 \dot{A}_i(\vec{k},t)\\ = -\epsilon_0 E_i(\vec{k},t)Πi​(k)=∂A˙i∗​(k,t)∂L(k,t)​=ϵ0​A˙i​(k,t)=−ϵ0​Ei​(k,t)

So the conjugate momentum to the vector potential is the electric field.

The normal modes

The hamiltonian above looks roughly like a harmonic oscillator, but not really yet as there are some funny conjugates trailing. This can get solved through the definition of the appropiate raising and lowering operators, named normal modes. They are defined through:

a^i(k⃗)=ϵ0ω2ℏ(A^i(k⃗)+iϵ0ωΠ^i)a^i†(k⃗)=ϵ0ω2ℏ(A^i†(k⃗)−iϵ0ωΠ^i†)\hat{a}_i(\vec{k}) = \sqrt{\frac{\epsilon_0 \omega}{2\hbar }}\left(\hat{A}_i(\vec{k})+\frac{i}{\epsilon_0\omega}\hat{\Pi}_i\right)\\ \hat{a}^\dag_i(\vec{k}) = \sqrt{\frac{\epsilon_0 \omega}{2\hbar }}\left(\hat{A}^\dag_i(\vec{k})-\frac{i}{\epsilon_0\omega}\hat{\Pi}_i^\dag \right)a^i​(k)=2ℏϵ0​ω​​(A^i​(k)+ϵ0​ωi​Π^i​)a^i†​(k)=2ℏϵ0​ω​​(A^i†​(k)−ϵ0​ωi​Π^i†​)

With the underlying commutation relationships of the conjugate operators we obtain the usual raising and lowering operators:

 [a^i(k⃗),a^j†(k⃗′)]=δijδ(k⃗−k⃗′)~[\hat{a}_i(\vec{k}), \hat{a}^\dag_j(\vec{k}')] =\delta_{ij}\delta(\vec{k}-\vec{k}') [a^i​(k),a^j†​(k′)]=δij​δ(k−k′)

The notion of the photon

We can now get back to the interpretation of the eigenstates of the raising and lowering operators as a photon. We have seen previously in lecture 6 that the operator n^i(k⃗)=a^i†(k⃗)a^i(k⃗)\hat{n}_i(\vec{k}) = \hat{a}_i^\dag (\vec{k}) \hat{a}_i(\vec{k})n^i​(k)=a^i†​(k)a^i​(k) is counting the occupation number in the Fock basis:

The nin_ini​ are then non-negative integers. We can further create a well-defined photon number state through the raising operators from the vacuum:
