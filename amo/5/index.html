The electric dipole approximation

Below you see the interaction between an atom and an electromagnetic wave E⃗\vec{E}E with wave vector k⃗\vec{k}k. The states |g>\text{|g>}|g> and |e>\text{|e>}|e> stand for the ground and excited state and ℏω0\hbar\omega_0ℏω0​ is the energy of the resonant transition between the states.

We consider an atom which is located in a radiation field. By resonant coupling with the frequency ω0\omega_0ω0​, it can go from the ground state ∣g⟩\left|g\right\rangle∣g⟩ to the excited state ∣e⟩\left|e\right\rangle∣e⟩.

The potential energy of a charge distribution in a homogeneous electromagnetic field E⃗\vec{E}E is:

Epot=∑iqir⃗i⋅E⃗.E_\text{pot} = \sum_i q_i \vec{r}_i\cdot \vec{E}.Epot​=i∑​qi​ri​⋅E.

If the upper limit of the sum is 2, we obtain the dipole moment

For the hydrogen atom, the distance corresponds to the Bohr radius.

Selection rules

We can now look into the allowed transition in the atom as they are what we will typically observe within experiments.

Change of parity

The parity operator is defined as:

P^ψ(r⃗)=ψ(−r⃗)\hat{P}\psi(\vec{r}) = \psi(-\vec{r})P^ψ(r)=ψ(−r)

For the eigenfunction we have:

P^ψ(r⃗)=λψ(r⃗)λ=±1\hat{P} \psi(\vec{r}) = \lambda \psi(\vec{r})\\ \lambda = \pm 1P^ψ(r)=λψ(r)λ=±1

The eigenvalues are called odd and even. From the definition of the dipole operator we can see that it is of odd parity. What about the parity of the states that it is coupling ? If they have both the same parity than the whole integral will disappear and no dipole transition can appear.

We can become more concrete for the given eigenfunctions as we have within spherical coordinates:

(r,θ,ϕ)→(r,π−θ,ϕ+π)(r, \theta, \phi) \rightarrow (r, \pi -\theta, \phi+\pi)(r,θ,ϕ)→(r,π−θ,ϕ+π)

For the orbitals of the hydrogen atom we then have explicitly:

P^ψnlm(r,θ,ϕ)=Rnl(r)Ylm(π−θ,ϕ+π)=(−1)lRnl(r)Ylm(,θ,ϕ)\hat{P}\psi_{nlm}(r, \theta, \phi) = R_{nl}(r)Y_{lm}(\pi -\theta, \phi+\pi)\\ = (-1)^l R_{nl}(r)Y_{lm}(, \theta, \phi)P^ψnlm​(r,θ,ϕ)=Rnl​(r)Ylm​(π−θ,ϕ+π)=(−1)lRnl​(r)Ylm​(,θ,ϕ)

This gives us the first selection rule that the orbital angular momentum has to change for dipole transitions Δl=±1\Delta l = \pm 1Δl=±1.

  • sss orbitals are only coupled to ppp orbitals through dipole transitions.

  • ppp orbitals are only coupled to sss and ddd orbitals through dipole transitions.

Circularly polarized light

Light has not just linear polarization, but it might also have some circular polarization. In this case we can write:

E⃗(t)=E02(cos⁡(ωt+φ)e⃗x+sin⁡(ωt+φ)e⃗y)E⃗(t)=Re(e⃗+E0e−iωt+ϕ)e⃗±=e⃗x±ie⃗y2\vec{E}(t) = \frac{E_0}{\sqrt{2}} \left(\cos(\omega t +\varphi)\vec{e}_x + \sin(\omega t +\varphi)\vec{e}_y\right)\\ \vec{E}(t) = \text{Re}\left(\vec{e}_+ E_0 e^{-i\omega t +\phi}\right)\\ \vec{e}_\pm = \frac{\vec{e}_x\pm i\vec{e}_y}{\sqrt{2}}E(t)=2​E0​​(cos(ωt+φ)ex​+sin(ωt+φ)ey​)E(t)=Re(e+​E0​e−iωt+ϕ)e±​=2​ex​±iey​​

So light with polarization ϵ⃗=e⃗+\vec{\epsilon} = \vec{e}_+ϵ=e+​ is called right-hand circular (σ+\sigma^+σ+) and ϵ⃗=e⃗−\vec{\epsilon} = \vec{e}_-ϵ=e−​ is called left-hand circular (σ−\sigma^-σ−). Let us now evaluate the transition elements here. The dipole operator element boils now down to the evaluation of the integral:

⟨l′,m′,n′∣x+iy∣l,m,n⟩\left\langle l',m',n'\right|x+iy\left|l,m,n\right\rangle⟨l′,m′,n′∣x+iy∣l,m,n⟩

Evaluation of the integrals lead now to the rule the projection of the quantum number has to change m′=m+1m' = m+1m′=m+1. In a similiar fashion we find for left-hand circular light the selection rule m′=m−1m' = m - 1m′=m−1.
