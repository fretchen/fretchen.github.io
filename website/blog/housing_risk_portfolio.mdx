In this blog post, I explore what actually protects your home when life goes wrong — and why paying off the mortgage faster isn't always the answer. To make the arguments easier to follow, they unfold as a conversation between three friends over dinner.

## Why extra repayment feels so safe

Sofia opens the door, slightly breathless. "Sorry — the oven timer. Come in."

It's a small reunion. The three met [on a ferry to Tunis](/blog/cosmopol_democracy) arguing about democracy until 3 AM — Sofia, who works at the European Commission in Brussels; Amara, a water governance researcher from Tunisia; and Adam, a Malta-based developer who can't resist building tools nobody asked for.

It's Adam and Amara's first time seeing the apartment. Living room with a view of the park. Compact kitchen with new tiles. Then the bathroom — old tiles, cracked mirror, fixtures from 2003.

"Are you going to redo this?" Adam asks.

"Eventually. Every spare euro goes into the mortgage."

Over dinner, she explains. She's been making extra repayments beyond the regular rate since she moved in. "Every payment feels like buying another piece of freedom. One day this place is _mine_. No bank, no debt."

"I don't understand people who invest while they still owe money," she adds. "Pay off the house first. Then you're safe."

Amara is quiet.

"You okay?" Sofia asks.

"Can I tell you something?"

## The money in the walls

"My research contract wasn't renewed." Amara says it matter-of-factly. "Budget cuts in the water governance program. Six months without income."

"I had a small apartment in Tunis," she continues. "Nothing fancy — two rooms near the Medina. My base between research trips. I was putting every spare dinar into extra repayment. The balance was dropping beautifully."

"Month two without a paycheck, I couldn't cover the rate. The bank told me the payments I'd already made were gone — I couldn't get them back. The money was in the walls. And I needed it in my account."

"I almost lost the apartment. My sister lent me money — barely enough."

Sofia: "Wouldn't the bank give you a payment pause?"

"They did — two months. But with no income _and_ a repair bill, two months isn't enough. That's what I learned: it's never just one thing."

Sofia: "But you _did_ save on interest. That's not nothing."

"Saving on interest is real, and it's better than any savings account. But that's a _return_ question. What would have protected my apartment wasn't a lower balance. It was cash I could actually use when everything went sideways."

She looks at Sofia's bathroom door, still open down the hallway.

"The money in the walls can't help you. The money outside the walls can."

Adam: "So — don't pay off the mortgage?"

"_Sequence matters_. Safety net first. Extra repayment second. I had the order wrong."

<details>
<summary>🔬 The return question vs. the risk question</summary>

Saving on mortgage interest is equivalent to earning a guaranteed return — that's real and valuable. The question "should I invest at higher but uncertain returns instead?" is a _return comparison_.

But as we explored in [the ETF diversification post](/blog/etf_diversification_interactive), reliably estimating future returns requires 80–100 years of data (Merton, 1980). We're genuinely bad at it. What we _can_ measure is risk. This post focuses on what protects your home — not what earns more. The interest-saving argument is valid; it's just answering a different question.

</details>

## What's actually on your balance sheet?

Amara pulls out her phone. "After the crisis, I kept staring at my finances trying to understand what went wrong. I'd never looked at the whole picture."

She shows Sofia a simple view: apartment value on one side, mortgage on the other, a thin sliver of savings in the corner.

"The house dominates everything," Sofia says.

"Right. But that's not what scared me — what scared me was how fast the thin slice of savings can vanish."

Sofia enters her own numbers. Apartment: €380,000. Mortgage: €290,000. Savings: €8,000.

Adam: "What if something breaks on top of that?"

Sofia: "That's what the savings are for."

Amara: "At my place it was a burst water pipe. Two weeks after the contract ended. €4,000."

Sofia does the math in her head. Then she tries the numbers on Amara's phone.

## Where your financial risk actually comes from

Adam noticed something in the numbers. The mortgage — the thing Sofia feared most — was the most predictable part of her finances. Fixed rate, fixed monthly payment, known end date. It drops by the same amount every month, decade after decade. Meanwhile, apartment prices across European cities had risen sharply for years, then fell in a single correction. The mortgage balance during that time didn't flinch.

> Sofia: "So the debt is the boring part?"
>
> Adam: "The debt is the _safe_ part. The house price is where the surprises happen."

By _risk_ we mean how much the value of what you own — your apartment, your savings — can change from year to year. Not whether something _will_ go wrong, but how big the swings could be.

After her crisis, Amara had built a simple breakdown — a way to see where her financial risk actually came from. She'd discovered something that surprised her: almost all of it was the house. The mortgage, despite being the largest number on her balance sheet, contributed almost nothing. She showed it to Sofia.

> "The mortgage is... nothing?"
>
> "It's fixed. Zero surprise."

## So what do I do?

Sofia straightens up. "Okay. I have €300 a month after everything. What do I _do_ with it?"

Amara doesn't hesitate. "Buffer first. Every euro you put into extra repayment is an euro you can't get back in a crisis. Build six months of mortgage payments in reserves — _then_ accelerate the repayment."

"So it's not _don't pay off the mortgage_."

"It's _sequence_. Safety net first, extra payment second."

Sofia: "But those euros just sitting in a savings account earn nothing. At least extra repayment saves me 3.5%."

Amara: "It does. And once you have six months of buffer, every extra euro should go right back to repayment. But the first six months are worth more than any interest saving — because they're what keeps you in the apartment if something goes wrong."

Adam: "And once the buffer is full — right now 98% of your wealth is one apartment in Brussels. Even €100 a month into a broad savings plan means not everything depends on one market. I've [written about how that works](/blog/etf_diversification_interactive)."

"Of course you have," Amara says.

Sofia looks down the hallway at the bathroom.

"I'm keeping the extra repayment. But I'm building the buffer first."

She pauses. "And _then_ I'm fixing that bathroom."

## Three things to remember

1. **Your house is safe because you can _pay_ for it — not because you've _paid it off_.** The mortgage is predictable. What's unpredictable is life. A six-month buffer protects your home; a lower balance doesn't.

2. **The mortgage adds almost no financial risk.** It drops by the same amount every month — completely predictable. The actual uncertainty comes from your house price — and if you're staying, that risk is smaller than it looks because you save rent.

3. **Once the safety net is full, diversification beats concentration.** Your home is your biggest asset by far. A small, regular savings plan outside the house reduces your dependence on a single market. [Here's how that works in practice.](/blog/etf_diversification_interactive)

<summary>🔬 Technical appendix: How the risk is computed</summary>

The **RiskReality** widget computes each asset's contribution to your total financial uncertainty using a simplified **Contribution-to-Volatility** (CtV) decomposition.

**Inputs you provide:**
- Home value, mortgage remaining, cash savings, and investments

**Fixed parameters** (from [retail-portfolio-analysis](https://github.com/fretchen/retail-portfolio-analysis) notebooks NB05 + NB06b, based on GREIX Frankfurt quarterly data 2008–2022):
- Housing price volatility (mark-to-market): **σ = 7.5% p.a.**
- Housing price volatility (owner-occupier, after rent hedge): **σ = 4.2% p.a.** — because purchase prices and rents are strongly correlated (ρ ≈ 0.7, Sinai & Souleles 2005)
- Savings account volatility: **σ ≈ 0.5% p.a.**
- Investment portfolio volatility: **σ ≈ 12% p.a.** (diversified equity/bond mix)
- Mortgage volatility: **σ = 0%** (fixed-rate, perfectly predictable)

**Calculation:** For each asset, the variance contribution is (weight × volatility)². The widget shows the share of total variance per asset. Because the mortgage has zero volatility, it contributes nothing — even though it's a large number on your balance sheet. For typical European homeowners, housing represents 70–90% of total wealth (Gomes 2020), which is why it dominates the risk picture.

**"I'm staying" toggle:** Switches housing volatility from 7.5% to 4.2%. This reflects the economic insight that an owner-occupier is hedged against rent increases: if house prices fall, rents tend to fall too — but you're not paying rent, so you're unaffected. The effective risk is the _difference_ between price and rent movements (Yao & Zhang 2005).

**Why extra repayment doesn't reduce risk:** The mortgage has zero volatility regardless of its size. Reducing the balance changes your net worth but not your risk exposure — the house still moves by the same amount. This is the "crowding-out" effect: housing dominates the portfolio so completely that other decisions barely register (Cocco 2005).

**Limitations:**
- Cross-correlations between housing and savings are ignored (minimal for cash)
- GREIX data covers German cities — your local market may differ
- The rent hedge assumes you stay long-term; forced sellers face the full mark-to-market risk

**References:**
- Cocco, J.F. (2005). Portfolio Choice in the Presence of Housing. _Review of Financial Studies_, 18(2), 535–567.
- Gomes, F. (2020). Portfolio Choice Over the Life Cycle: A Survey. _Annual Review of Financial Economics_, 12, 277–304.
- Yao, R. & Zhang, H.H. (2005). Optimal Consumption and Portfolio Choices with Risky Housing and Borrowing Constraints. _Review of Financial Studies_, 18(1), 197–239.
- GREIX — German Real Estate Index. Housing price data for German cities.
