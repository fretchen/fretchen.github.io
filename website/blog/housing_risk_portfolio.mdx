In this blog post, three friends — Sofia, Adam, and Amara — have dinner and end up talking about mortgages, risk, and what "safe" actually means when you own a home. If you've met them before on a [ferry to Tunis](/blog/20) or during a [day of failed negotiations in Brussels](/blog/24), welcome back. If not, no worries — all you need to know is that they've been friends since a sleepless night arguing about democracy, and they haven't stopped arguing since.

## Sofia's apartment

Sofia opens the door, slightly breathless. "Sorry — the oven timer. Come in, come in."

The apartment smells of rosemary and something involving too much garlic. She gives Adam and Amara the tour — it's their first time here. Living room with a view of the park. Small balcony. The kitchen she's clearly proud of, new tiles and all.

Then the bathroom.

Old tiles. A mirror with a crack in the corner. Fixtures from the previous owner that were probably fashionable in 2003.

"Are you going to redo this?" Adam asks.

"Eventually." Sofia leans against the doorframe. "Right now every spare euro goes into the mortgage. I'd love to fix it, but the bathroom can wait — the debt can't."

Over dinner, she explains. She's been doing [Sondertilgung](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mortgage_loan#Prepayment) — extra repayments on top of the regular monthly rate — every month since she moved in. "Every payment feels like I'm buying another piece of freedom. No landlord can raise my rent. Nobody can kick me out. One day, this place is _mine_. Completely. No bank, no debt, nothing."

Adam nods. "That's a good feeling."

"It's the _best_ feeling." Sofia pours more wine. "I don't understand people who invest while they still owe money. Pay off the house first. Then you're safe."

Amara is quiet. She's been quiet since the bathroom.

Sofia notices. "You okay?"

Amara puts down her glass. "Can I tell you something?"

## The money in the walls

"My research contract wasn't renewed." Amara says it matter-of-factly, the way you describe something you've processed a hundred times. "Budget cuts in the water governance program. Six months without income."

Sofia and Adam both go still.

"I had an apartment too," Amara continues. "Smaller than this, but mine. I was doing everything right — every spare euro into extra repayment. My remaining balance was dropping beautifully. The spreadsheet looked perfect."

She pauses.

"Month two without a paycheck, I couldn't cover the rate. I called the bank. You know what they said?" She looks at Sofia. "'We can't give you back what you've already paid.' The money was in the walls. And I needed it in my account."

The table is silent.

"I almost lost the apartment. My sister lent me money — barely enough. But for three months I didn't know if I'd keep it."

Sofia stares at her wine. "But you _did_ save on interest, right? That's not nothing."

"I spent three months with a spreadsheet trying to convince myself of exactly that." Amara almost smiles. "Yes — saving 3.5% in interest is real, and it's better than any savings account. But that's a _return_ question. What would have protected my apartment wasn't a lower balance. It was cash. Money I could actually use when everything went sideways."

She looks at Sofia's bathroom door, still open down the hallway.

"The money in the walls can't help you. The money outside the walls can."

Adam leans back. "So you're saying don't pay off the mortgage?"

"No. I'm saying _sequence matters_. Safety net first. Extra repayment second. I just had the order wrong."

<details>
<summary>🔬 The return question vs. the risk question</summary>

Amara is making an important distinction here. Saving 3.5% on mortgage interest is equivalent to earning a 3.5% guaranteed return — that's real and valuable. The question "should I invest at 5–7% uncertain instead?" is a _return comparison_.

But as we explored in [the ETF diversification post](/blog/etf_diversification_interactive), reliably estimating future returns requires 80–100 years of data (Merton, 1980). We're genuinely bad at it. What we _can_ measure is risk. This post focuses on what protects your home — not what earns more. The interest-saving argument is valid; it's just answering a different question.

</details>

## What's actually on your balance sheet?

Amara pulls out her phone. "I kept staring at my finances afterward, trying to understand what happened. And the first thing I realized is — I'd never actually looked at the whole picture."

She shows Sofia a simple view. Everything in one image: the apartment value on one side, the mortgage on the other, the thin sliver of savings somewhere in the corner.

"The house dominates everything," Sofia says slowly.

"Right. And the mortgage" — Amara points — "is a known, predictable line going down. Fixed rate, fixed schedule. But the house value? The savings? Those can change overnight."

Sofia enters her own numbers. Her apartment: €380,000. Remaining mortgage: €290,000. Monthly savings beyond the mortgage: about €300. Existing savings: €8,000.

The picture is stark. The house is almost everything. The savings are a rounding error.

"Now," Amara says, "which of these can suddenly change without you having any say?"

## Your mortgage is boring — and that's good

Adam, who's been listening with increasing restlessness, leans forward. "Can I see that for a second?" He points at the balance sheet on Amara's phone. "Look at the mortgage line. It's a straight line going down. Fixed rate, fixed schedule, known end date. That's the most boring thing on here."

Sofia frowns. "Boring is bad?"

"Boring is _great_." Adam pulls out his laptop — because of course he does. "The mortgage is predictable. You know exactly what you owe next month and in ten years. Now look at this."

He pulls up housing price data. German cities, 2015–2023. Up 8% per year for six years. Then down 15% in a single correction. Brussels isn't that different.

"See the mortgage balance during that time?" he says. "Nothing. A calm line, completely unbothered. The house price went on a rollercoaster. The mortgage didn't blink."

He turns the laptop toward Sofia.

"The thing you _fear_ — the debt — is the predictable part. The thing you _trust_ — what the apartment is worth — is the uncertain part."

Sofia stares at the screen. "I never thought about it that way."

"Nobody does," Amara says quietly. "I didn't either."

## Where does your risk actually come from?

"Amara, you did the risk breakdown too, right?" Adam asks.

"Already did." She turns her phone around.

Adam grins. "...I'm so proud."

"Shut up."

What Amara shows is a simple breakdown: of all the uncertainty in Sofia's financial life, how much comes from the house, how much from savings, how much from everything else?

The answer is overwhelming. The house is almost all of it.

"Okay, but that changes once I pay it off, right?" Sofia says. "Less debt, less risk?"

"That's what I assumed too." Amara swipes. "Look — here's before paying off the mortgage, and here's after."

The risk barely changes.

Sofia blinks. "How?"

"Because the mortgage was never the risk," Amara says. "The mortgage is the boring, predictable straight line, remember? Paying it off removes a known quantity. The _unknown_ quantity — the house price — stays exactly the same."

Adam: "It's like canceling your alarm subscription but keeping the unlocked front door."

Amara: "That's... actually not bad."

"It makes your balance smaller," Amara continues. "It doesn't make the wobble smaller."

## Remember why you bought in the first place

A pause. Sofia stares at her plate. She looks deflated.

Amara reaches over and touches her arm. "Hey — remember why you bought this place?"

"So nobody can kick me out." Sofia's voice is smaller now. "So my rent can't go up. So I have something that's mine."

"And that instinct was dead right."

Sofia looks up.

"Think about it," Amara says. "Even now, with a mortgage, you don't pay rent. If rents in Brussels go up 30% over the next decade — and they might — you're completely unaffected. You locked in your housing cost the day you signed."

"In France they say buying means you stop _throwing money out the window_," Adam adds. "In Germany it's the peace of knowing what you'll pay next month. Across Europe, this is _the_ reason people buy."

"And it goes further than comfort," he continues, leaning forward. "Imagine house prices drop 20%. Sounds terrible, right? But what actually changes in your daily life?"

Sofia thinks. "...Nothing. I still live here. Same mortgage payment."

"Exactly. The rent protection you bought is also a risk dampener. A crash in house prices doesn't change your monthly reality. Renters whose landlord raises the price? They feel it immediately. You don't."

Amara pulls up the risk breakdown again and toggles something. "Look — this is the raw housing risk. And _this_ is your actual risk after accounting for the fact that you live in the place and don't pay rent."

The bar shrinks. Noticeably.

Sofia exhales. "So the risk is real, but smaller than it looked — because I bought for the right reason."

"Your instinct to buy was sound," Amara says. "It's more sound than you knew."

## The €300/month question

Sofia straightens up. She's practical — once she understands a problem, she wants a solution. "Okay. I have about €300 a month after everything. What do I actually _do_ with it?"

Amara and Adam look at each other.

Adam reaches for his laptop. Amara raises an eyebrow.

"What?" Adam says. "I'm just helping."

"You're making a tool."

"...I'm making a tool."

Sofia laughs — the first real laugh of the evening. "Fine. Make your tool."

What they sketch out together is simple. A slider: how much of the €300 goes to Sondertilgung, how much to a liquid safety reserve, and — once the reserve is large enough — how much to a diversified savings plan?

Two things matter:

On the left: **months of safety**. How many months of mortgage payments could Sofia cover from her liquid reserves if her income disappeared tomorrow? At €1,200/month mortgage and €8,000 in savings, she has about 6.5 months. Not bad — but every euro going into Sondertilgung is a euro _not_ growing that buffer.

On the right: **risk concentration**. As liquid reserves or a diversified portfolio grow alongside the fixed house, the housing concentration shrinks. Not because the house gets smaller — because there's now a counterweight.

Sofia moves the slider. When everything goes to Sondertilgung, her months of safety stay flat. When she splits — €150 to savings, €150 to mortgage — the safety buffer grows steadily.

"The first euros into reserves have the biggest impact," Amara says. "Once you're in the green — six months covered — then Sondertilgung or diversification both make sense."

"So it's not _don't pay off the mortgage_," Sofia says.

"It's _buffer first_," Amara confirms. "Then tilgen to your heart's content."

## Two homeowners, same apartment

Amara leans back. "Let me make it really concrete. Two homeowners. Same apartment, same mortgage, same monthly income. One puts everything extra into Sondertilgung for five years. The other builds a six-month buffer first and saves the rest."

"Then what?" Sofia asks.

"Job loss. Eight months."

Adam winces.

"Homeowner A has a lower remaining balance — great on paper. But no cash. Month two, the rate bounces. Month three, the bank sends letters. Month five, she's looking at selling."

"Homeowner B has €15,000 in reserves. Covers the mortgage for the full eight months. Finds a new job. Keeps the apartment."

"After the crisis, Homeowner A was forced to sell at a loss — in a down market, because _everyone_ sells when they need cash. Homeowner B kept her home and eventually resumed the Sondertilgung."

She looks at Sofia.

"The safest path isn't the lowest debt. It's the most options when life gets hard."

Sofia is quiet for a long moment. "That's basically what happened to you."

Amara nods. "Except nobody told me."

## Clearing the table

They stack plates. Adam carries glasses to the kitchen. The conversation has drifted to lighter things — Adam's latest regulatory headache, Amara's new research position, whether Brussels or Tunis has better coffee.

At the door, coats halfway on, Sofia says: "Okay. What I'm taking away."

She counts on her fingers.

"One. My house is safe because I can _pay_ for it — not because I've _paid it off_. Six months of reserves first. Then Sondertilgung."

Amara: "Two. Your mortgage is the boring, predictable part. The actual uncertainty is your house price — and you already have built-in protection because you don't pay rent."

Adam: "Three. Once the safety net is full, diversification gives your finances a second leg. A small monthly savings plan — bonds, international stocks — shrinks the concentration." He pauses. "I can send you both a link."

"Of course you can," Amara says.

"It's a [good link](/blog/etf_diversification_interactive)," Adam insists.

Sofia looks down the hallway at the bathroom. Old tiles, cracked mirror, fixtures from 2003.

"I'm keeping the Sondertilgung," she says. "But I'm building the buffer first."

She pauses.

"And _then_ I'm fixing that bathroom."

Amara smiles. "That's all I wanted to hear."
