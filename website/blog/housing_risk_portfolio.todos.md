# Critique: What Actually Protects Your Home?

**Target audience:** Risk-averse (female) European homeowner, 30–50, who has a mortgage and believes paying it off fast is the safest strategy. Skeptical of stocks, associates investing with gambling. Likely hasn't read the ETF post.
**Overall impression:** The dialogue format works well — Sofia is a convincing proxy for the reader, and Amara's personal story is the strongest section. The core thesis (buffer first, tilgen second) lands clearly. However, the middle sections are repetitive (three consecutive sections making variations of "mortgage is predictable, house price isn't"), the characters are never introduced, and some claims would break trust with a German homeowner who knows how banks actually work.

## Critical Issues

- [ ] **[§ Sofia's apartment]** The AUTHOR COMMENT flags it, but it's unsolved: Sofia, Adam, and Amara appear with zero introduction. A new reader has no idea who these people are, what they do, or why they're having dinner together. The cosmopol_democracy post introduces each character with occupation and context. This post drops three names and expects the reader to care. The target audience — who probably hasn't read the earlier posts — will feel dropped into someone else's conversation.

- [ ] **[§ Introduction]** "It builds on the risk budget approach from the ETF post" — the target audience doesn't know what a "risk budget" is and likely hasn't read that post. This sentence signals "this post isn't for me, it's for people who already know about investing." The ETF post intro works precisely because it doesn't reference prerequisites — it starts with "a simple approach to invest money." This intro should do the same for housing risk.

- [ ] **[§ The money in the walls]** Amara's bank call is oversimplified in a way that would undermine trust with German homeowners. German banks routinely offer Tilgungssatzwechsel (reducing the repayment rate), Stundung (temporary payment pause), or Ratenpause before refusing outright. A reader who has actually called her bank about financial difficulties will know this and think: "That's not how it works." The scenario needs to acknowledge these options exist but explain why they may not be enough (delays, conditions, limited duration).

- [ ] **[§ Your mortgage is boring / § Where does your risk actually come from?]** These two sections make the same argument twice: the mortgage is predictable, the house price is uncertain, therefore paying off the mortgage doesn't reduce the real risk. The reader gets the point after "Your mortgage is boring" — then has to sit through the same logic again with a risk breakdown she can't yet interact with (the widget is a placeholder). This is where the target audience's attention would drop. One of these sections should be compressed into the other.

## Suggestions

- [ ] **[§ The money in the walls]** The target audience would immediately think: "But I have Restschuldversicherung / Berufsunfähigkeitsversicherung." Many German homeowners buy payment protection insurance precisely for the job-loss scenario. Not addressing this makes the argument feel like it only works for people who didn't plan ahead. Amara could acknowledge she didn't have it, or that it has limitations (waiting periods, payout caps, exclusions).

- [ ] **[§ Where does your risk actually come from?]** "Risk contribution breakdown" is used but never concretely explained. The reader is told "of all the uncertainty in your financial life, how much comes from the house" — but uncertainty about *what*? Price dropping? Monthly costs changing? The phrase "risk contribution" is finance jargon. For this audience, it needs a one-sentence concrete translation, something like: "If your total financial situation were to wobble, how much of that wobble comes from house prices vs. everything else?"

- [ ] **[§ Two homeowners, same apartment]** This section essentially retells Amara's own story from §2 — same scenario (job loss), same outcome (forced to sell vs. kept the apartment). The reader already heard this. The section either needs to be cut (since Amara's personal story is more compelling) or reframed with a genuinely different scenario (illness, divorce, major repair).

- [ ] **[§ The €300/month question]** "Risk concentration" and "diversified portfolio" appear here without explanation. For a reader who is skeptical of stocks, "diversified portfolio" triggers the exact reaction Sofia voiced earlier: "I don't understand people who invest while they still owe money." The section handles the buffer argument well but slips in investing language without Sofia (the proxy skeptic) pushing back on it. She should resist this step more — it's the hardest sell for this audience.

- [ ] **[§ Clearing the table]** Adam's third takeaway — "diversification gives your finances a second leg" — feels like a sales pitch. The target audience was just told this post is about protection, not returns. Then the final takeaway is "buy bonds and international stocks." This risks losing the reader at the very end. Consider whether Adam's point should be softer: "keep the reserves somewhere they won't lose value" rather than jumping to a specific investment recommendation.

- [ ] **[§ The money in the walls]** Sofia has €8,000 in savings and a €1,200/month mortgage — that's already 6.5 months of buffer. Later in §7 the post says "not bad." But the entire argument is premised on the danger of having no buffer. Sofia already *has* a decent buffer. This weakens the urgency. Either Sofia's numbers need to show a thinner buffer (e.g. €3,000 savings), or the post should acknowledge that she's in a reasonable position and the point is about *protecting* that buffer rather than building one from scratch.

- [ ] **[§ Throughout]** All three characters speak in a similar analytical register. Adam and Amara in particular are hard to tell apart — both make data-driven observations with dry wit. In the earlier posts, Adam is the tech-pragmatist and Amara is the frustrated practitioner. Here they blend together. Giving them more distinct voices (Adam more blunt/direct, Amara more reflective/personal) would help the dialogue feel like three real people.

## Nitpicks

- [ ] **[§ Two homeowners, same apartment]** "Month five, she's looking at selling" — the post aims for gender-neutral framing through dialogue, but slips back to "she" here. Either use "they" or name the homeowner.

- [ ] **[§ Your mortgage is boring]** "Adam pulls out his laptop — because of course he does" — this running gag works for returning readers but means nothing to new readers. A brief "Adam, who builds tools the way other people doodle, reaches for his laptop" would serve both groups.

- [ ] **[§ Sofia's apartment]** The Sondertilgung Wikipedia link goes to the generic "Mortgage loan#Prepayment" section, which is in English and covers US/UK mortgage concepts. For a European audience, this link is unhelpful — the concept is specifically German. Either link to the German Wikipedia article for Sondertilgung or explain it inline without a link (the inline explanation is already clear enough).

- [ ] **[§ References]** The references section lists academic papers (Cocco 2005, Gomes 2020, Yao & Zhang 2005) but none of them are cited in the text. For this audience, unlabeled academic references at the end feel performative. Either cite them where they support a specific claim, or drop the section.

- [ ] **[§ Remember why you bought in the first place]** "In France they say buying means you stop throwing money out the window" — Adam states this as common European knowledge, but the target audience (a German homeowner) might not know the French expression. It works as color but should feel like Adam sharing something, not assuming the reader knows it — which it mostly does through dialogue, just borderline.

- [ ] **[§ Introduction]** "To make the different perspectives tangible, the arguments unfold as a conversation between three friends over dinner" — the word "tangible" is slightly academic for this audience. "relatable" or "easier to follow" would be more natural.
