# Critique: What Actually Protects Your Home?

**Target audience:** Risk-averse European homeowner, 30–50, who has a mortgage and believes paying it off fast is the safest strategy. Skeptical of stocks, associates investing with gambling. Likely hasn't read the ETF post.

**Overall impression:** The post's structure is now sound: Amara's lived crisis → ShockCalculator (visceral) → risk decomposition → RiskReality (analytical) → practical advice → takeaways. The two interactive widgets each land a distinct punch. The biggest remaining problems are (1) the author comments flag real issues — section titles, dialog-heaviness, the Germany data tangent, and "wobble" terminology all need fixing, (2) the post's middle section ("The boring mortgage") introduces concepts clumsily, and (3) the dialogue still needs narrator passages to keep a non-fiction reader oriented. The ending ("So what do I do?" + "Three things to remember") is now clean and well-linked.

---

## Critical Issues

- [ ] **[§ The boring mortgage — AUTHOR COMMENT: too dialog-heavy]** The author is right. From "The boring mortgage" onward, the post reads like a novel transcript. The target audience expects blog-post narration with dialogue as illustration — not pure screenplay. The ETF post shows the pattern: a paragraph explains the concept, then characters react. Here, the core insight ("the mortgage contributes almost nothing to your financial risk; the house dominates") is buried in rapid-fire dialogue with no narrator framing. **Fix:** For each key insight in this section, add 1–2 sentences of narrator exposition *before* the dialogue illustrates it. Example: "Adam pointed out something Sofia hadn't considered. The mortgage — the thing she feared most — was the most predictable line on her balance sheet. [Then dialogue]." This applies throughout the post, but is most acute in this section.

- [ ] **[§ The boring mortgage — AUTHOR COMMENT: "mortgage line" unclear]** The author is right. "Look at the mortgage line on your balance sheet" presumes the reader sees a visual that doesn't exist in the post. There's no chart or image at this point — just dialogue referencing one. The reader doesn't know what "the mortgage line" means. **Fix options:** (a) Add a simple static chart: mortgage balance (straight line) vs. house price (volatile) over time, as originally planned. (b) Or rewrite the passage to not reference a visual: "Your mortgage drops by the same amount every month — it's completely predictable. Your house price? That's a different story."

- [ ] **[§ The boring mortgage — AUTHOR COMMENT: Germany data is random]** The author is right. Sofia lives in Brussels. Amara's story is set in Tunis. Adam is based in Malta. Nobody has a connection to Germany. Pulling up "German cities, 2015–2023" housing data breaks immersion — the reader will wonder why. The data comes from GREIX (German index), which is the real source for the analysis, but the narrative shouldn't expose this arbitrarily. **Fix options:** (a) Use Brussels/Belgian housing data if available — even approximate ("Brussels apartment prices doubled, then dropped 10%"). (b) Use generic European framing: "housing prices across European cities" without naming Germany. (c) Have Adam say "I pulled up some numbers for European cities" — the technical appendix already cites GREIX for the curious.

- [ ] **[§ Throughout — AUTHOR COMMENT: "wobble" terminology]** The author is right, and this is a consistency issue. The ETF post uses "risk" and "volatility" throughout, and the technical appendix of this very post uses "uncertainty" and "volatility." The word "wobble" appears 12 times in the RiskReality widget and throughout the narrative. It reads as condescending for the target audience (educated 30–50 professionals) and creates a terminology mismatch with the linked ETF post. **Fix:** Replace "wobble" with "risk" everywhere (narrative + widget). Where "risk" is first used in the financial sense, add one clarifying sentence: "By risk we mean how much your financial picture can change from year to year — not whether something *will* go wrong, but how much it *could* move." This aligns with the ETF post and respects the audience's intelligence.

- [ ] **[§ Section titles — AUTHOR COMMENT: titles too scenic]** The author is right. Current titles ("Sofia's apartment", "The money in the walls", "The boring mortgage and the risky house") read like chapter titles in a novel. The target audience scanning the page needs informational titles that promise a payoff. **Fix:** Replace with titles that tell the reader what they'll learn. Options:
  - "Sofia's apartment" → "Why extra repayment feels so safe" or "The Sondertilgung instinct"
  - "The money in the walls" → "What happens when income stops" or "When the crisis hit"
  - "What's actually on your balance sheet?" → OK as-is (it's a question that promises an answer)
  - "The boring mortgage and the risky house" → "Where your financial risk actually comes from"
  - "So what do I do?" → "Buffer first, then repay" or OK as-is

## Suggestions

- [ ] **[§ The money in the walls]** Amara's story is set in Tunis — consistent with her character — but the target audience (European homeowner) will think: "My bank would offer a payment pause (Stundung/Ratenpause) before letting me default." The post doesn't acknowledge this. **Fix:** Add a one-line exchange — Sofia: "Wouldn't the bank give you a pause?" / Amara: "They did, for two months. But with no income and a repair bill, two months isn't enough." This makes the crisis feel realistic for the European reader and bridges directly to the ShockCalculator's "two shocks at once" argument.

- [ ] **[§ The boring mortgage]** The section tries to deliver two insights in rapid dialogue: (1) the mortgage is predictable/boring, (2) the house dominates financial risk. These are distinct ideas but blur together. **Fix:** Split into two clear beats. Beat 1 (narrator + short dialogue): "The mortgage — fixed rate, fixed schedule — is the most predictable part of your finances." Beat 2 (narrator → intro to RiskReality): "So where does the actual risk come from? Almost entirely from one thing: what your home is worth."

- [ ] **[§ The boring mortgage]** "Amara swipes to a new view on her phone — a single horizontal bar." This narrative transition into the RiskReality widget is abrupt. The reader doesn't know what they're about to see. The ETF post handles widget transitions better: it explains what the tool does *before* showing it. **Fix:** Add a framing sentence before the widget: "Amara had built a simple breakdown after her crisis — a way to see where the financial risk actually comes from. She showed Sofia."

- [ ] **[§ What's actually on your balance sheet?]** The section transitions from "your wealth is mostly housing" straight to "what if something breaks?" without connecting the two. These are different insights. **Fix:** Add a bridge line: "The house dominates. But that's not what scared me — what scared me was how fast the thin slice of savings can vanish."

- [ ] **[§ So what do I do?]** Sofia accepts the buffer argument immediately. But the target audience's strongest objection is: "Money sitting in a savings account earns almost nothing — I'd rather save on 3.5% mortgage interest." Sofia should voice this objection once for authenticity. **Fix:** Add: Sofia: "But those euros just sitting in a savings account earn nothing. At least extra repayment saves me 3.5%." / Amara: "It does. And after six months of buffer, every extra euro should go right back to repayment. But the first six months of reserves are worth more than any interest saving — because they're what keeps you in the apartment."

- [ ] **[§ So what do I do?]** Adam's diversification pitch ("A small monthly savings plan — bonds, international stocks — gives your finances a second leg") still reads slightly like a sales pitch. The phrase "second leg" is vague. **Fix:** Make it concrete and brief: "Right now 98% of your wealth is one apartment. Even €100/month into a broad ETF means not everything depends on Brussels real estate." Then the link to the ETF post.

- [ ] **[§ Three things to remember]** Point 2 uses "straight line going down" — this metaphor assumes the reader has seen a chart that doesn't exist in the post. Either add the chart earlier (see Critical Issue above) or rephrase: "It drops by the same amount every month — completely predictable."

- [ ] **[§ Technical appendix]** Still uses "wobble" in quotes and as terminology ("How the wobble is computed"). Should be updated to match whatever term replaces "wobble" in the main text.

- [ ] **[§ Technical appendix]** Mentions "Inputs you provide: Home value, mortgage remaining, liquid savings" but the widget now has four inputs (Home value, Mortgage remaining, Cash savings, Investments). Update to match.

- [ ] **[§ References]** Academic papers (Cocco 2005, Gomes 2020, Yao & Zhang 2005) are listed but never cited in the text. For this audience, uncited references feel performative. **Fix:** Either cite them where they support claims (in the `<details>` blocks), or remove the section and keep only the technical appendix which already references the data sources.

- [ ] **[§ RiskReality widget]** The widget now has an "Investments (ETFs etc.)" field defaulting to €0. This is good for the target audience (Sofia has no ETFs). But the "Annual wobble" column in the breakdown table will confuse readers who haven't been told what "annual wobble" means. If "wobble" is replaced with "risk," this becomes "Annual risk" — which still needs a one-line explanation (tooltip or footnote): "How much this asset's value could swing in a typical year."

## Nitpicks

- [ ] **[§ Introduction]** "In this blog post, I explore what actually protects your home" — the ETF post uses an identical pattern ("In this blog post, I present a simple, systematic approach..."). This is fine for consistency but reads slightly formulaic. Consider a more direct hook: "What actually protects your home when life goes wrong? Not what you'd think."

- [ ] **[§ The money in the walls]** "Every spare dinar" — accurate for Tunis, but "dinar" might momentarily confuse the target audience (European homeowner who thinks in euros). No fix needed, but consider whether to add a parenthetical or just let context carry it.

- [ ] **[§ References]** If kept, consider moving them into the `<details>` technical appendix — that's where the curious reader would look.
