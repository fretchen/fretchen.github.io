# Critique: What Actually Protects Your Home?

**Target audience:** Risk-averse European homeowner, 30–50, who has a mortgage and believes paying it off fast is the safest strategy. Skeptical of stocks, associates investing with gambling. Likely hasn't read the ETF post.
**Overall impression:** Post has improved significantly. Characters are introduced, the two repetitive sections are merged, the redundant "two homeowners" section is cut, and Amara's story is now grounded in Tunis (consistent with her character). The ShockCalculator widget in §3 is the strongest interactive element — it makes the buffer argument visceral. Adam's closing pitch is softer. Remaining issues are mostly about bridging transitions, tightening jargon, and adding one missing counterargument.

## Suggestions

- [ ] **[§ What's actually on your balance sheet?]** The section starts with balance sheet ("apartment value on one side, mortgage on the other") then jumps to "What if something breaks?" without a bridge. These are two different insights: (1) your wealth is almost all housing, (2) your savings vanish under two shocks. **Fix:** Add a transition line from Amara like: "The house dominates everything. But that's not what scared me — what scared me was how fast the thin slice of savings disappeared."

- [ ] **[§ The boring mortgage]** "Risk breakdown" and "uncertainty" remain unexplained jargon. The reader is told "of all the uncertainty in Sofia's financial life, the house is almost all of it" — uncertainty about what? **Fix:** One concrete sentence: "If your financial situation were to wobble — house prices falling, savings losing value — how much of that wobble comes from the house vs. everything else?"

- [ ] **[§ The €300 question]** "Concentration" is finance jargon the target audience won't use. "as liquid reserves grow, the housing share of her total wealth shrinks" would be clearer as "how much of your wealth is locked in one single thing."

- [ ] **[§ The €300 question]** Sofia accepts the buffer argument with zero pushback. But the target audience thinks: "Why let money sit when I could be saving on interest?" Sofia should resist at least once — e.g., "But those euros sitting in a savings account earn nothing" — and Amara or Adam should answer that directly. This is the hardest sell for this audience and currently the weakest moment.

- [ ] **[§ The €300 question]** Sofia's numbers: €8,000 savings / €1,200 rate = 6.5 months. The ShockCalculator makes this work (one shock = tight, two = red). But the text says "every euro into extra repayment keeps that buffer flat" — implying she needs *more*, when 6.5 months already shows green. Either reduce savings to €3,000–5,000 (making defaults more dramatic) or reframe: "Good — but only if nothing else goes wrong at the same time."

- [ ] **[§ References]** Academic papers (Cocco 2005, Gomes 2020, Yao & Zhang 2005) still listed at the end but never cited in the text. For this audience, unlabeled references feel performative. Either cite them where they support a claim (e.g., in the `<details>` block), or drop the section.

- [ ] **[§ Throughout]** Adam and Amara still sound similar in §4 ("The boring mortgage"). Both make analytical observations in the same register. Amara's voice is more personal elsewhere (her story, "that's what I assumed"), but in §4 she slips into Adam's data mode. Give her reflective phrasing ("That's what I assumed" works — extend this pattern) and keep Adam blunt/punchy ("It's like canceling your alarm subscription").
