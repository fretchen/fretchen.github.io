Over the last year, I really enjoyed diving a bit deeper into the prisoner's dilemma.  Interestingly, I recently understood that its quantum version provides a beautiful connection to the role of institutions, which a central pillar in modern political economics. 
What follows will be my attempt to explain the connection from the economics side because it seems more consequential than the physics perspective.

## The basics of the prisoner's dilemma

I already wrote a full post on the [prisoner's dilemma](/blog/13) with Walter White and Jesse Pinkman, so is a quick summary wich the essential information that is important to this post. Jesse and Walter have been arrested and are interrogated separately, each offered the same deal: betray your partner and testify for immunity, or stay loyal and say nothing.

The sentences follow from both choices:

- Both stay loyal: 3 years each. 
- Both betray: 5 years each. 
- But if one betrays while the other stays loyal, the betrayer walks free while the loyal partner takes 15 years.

That last outcome is the crux of the dilemma. Whatever your partner does, betraying looks better — so both betray, and both end up worse off than if they had trusted each other. 

This leaves the question: what would it take to make loyalty the rational choice? 

## What an institution could look like here

The economics literature keeps returning to the same answer: an **institution** the players route their decisions through instead of acting in isolation. It does two things, and both are essential:

- First, it couples their choices: rather than chatting freely, each player submits a single move, and the mediator links the two by a rule fixed in advance. 
- Second, it enforces that rule — whatever it dictates becomes the sentence, with no appeal. 


In the prisoners dilemma, that institution has a face: **Saul Goodman**, the lawyer both Walter and Jesse hire. And Saul is not a neutral bystander — a client who testifies unravels the whole operation and buries Saul along with it, so he has every reason to make loyalty each man's own best move. Here is how he arranges it. Before the interrogations, Saul prepares a single defense file holding two sworn statements — one for Walter, one for Jesse — both drafted to *deny everything*. Each client then privately leaves Saul one standing instruction for what to do with the statements. Saul carries out the instructions, files the result with the court, and the court hands down sentences by the original deal. The two men never speak; everything routes through Saul.

Crucially, Saul offers a **third instruction** beyond staying loyal and betraying. Call it **Commit** — a commitment device in Schelling's sense: a move you lock in ahead of time that binds your fate to your partner's. Isn't adding an option just cheating, though — changing the game? It is changing the game, deliberately — but Saul offers it symmetrically to both clients and rewrites none of the original sentences, and adding an enforceable action like this is *exactly* what building an institution means.

Each instruction just tells Saul how to edit the file, which starts with both statements denying everything. And switching a statement twice returns it to the original — that toggle is what makes the third instruction work:

- **Stay loyal** — file my statement as drafted (deny everything).
- **Betray** — switch my statement to testimony against him, and grab the immunity.
- **Commit** — switch both statements at once, his and mine together.

That "flip both" move is the whole trick, and it is worth playing with. Play with it for a moment and the logic of **Commit** appears. If you Commit and your partner betrays, your "flip both" cancels their "flip mine" and lands the flip on *their* letter instead — the scorecard now reads that *they* betrayed and *you* stayed loyal, so you walk free and they take the 15 years. If you both Commit, the two "flip both" moves cancel entirely and the scorecard stays at mutual loyalty. Here is the full table the mediator enforces, in prison years (your sentence first, your partner's second; lower is better):

|  | They stay loyal | They betray | They Commit |
|---|---|---|---|
| **You stay loyal** | 3, 3 | 15, 0 | 5, 5 |
| **You betray** | 0, 15 | 5, 5 | 15, 0 |
| **You Commit** | 5, 5 | 0, 15 | 3, 3 |

Now look at what happened to the trap. Against a committer, betraying is punished automatically — you jump straight to 15 years. So neither player wants to deviate from *both committing*: that gives each of them just 3 years, while going loyal drops you to the 5-year mutual-betrayal outcome and betraying costs you 15. Mutual loyalty has become the stable, self-interested choice. The institution didn't preach; it just made the punishment for betrayal credible, and built it into the rules.

## Making the connection to the quantum game

At this stage you can make a connection that still strikes me as fairly deep. The exact game we just described — the two lines, the referee's bracket, the "flip both" move — is also known, in a completely different language, to the quantum-physics community.

That picture above is a **quantum circuit**. The two lines are two qubits. The referee's bracket is the entangling operation physicists call J (before) and J† (after). The three moves are single-qubit gates: Cooperate is doing nothing, Defect is a bit-flip, and **Commit is a phase-flip** — the move physicists write as Q̂. Run the quantum mechanics and you get exactly the payoff table above. This is the celebrated Eisert–Wilkens–Lewenstein construction, and its headline result is precisely the one we found by hand: entanglement lets the players escape the prisoner's dilemma.

What I find striking is how thoroughly the mystery dissolves once you notice the equivalence. There is a known result — van Enk & Pike, *Physical Review A*, 2002 — showing that this discrete quantum game is *mathematically identical* to the classical mediated game we built with a scorecard and a referee. The entanglement isn't doing anything a trusted enforcer couldn't do. In the quantum version, the laws of physics play the role of the Leviathan: unitarity is the sanction, and it executes the punishment automatically, simultaneously, with no communication and no one to bribe.

## Connecting the dots and outlook

Once you see the referee for what it is, the quantum game stops being exotic and starts rhyming with a lot of economics.

The "Commit" move is essentially a **commitment device**. In mechanism design there is a clean version of this called *program equilibrium* (Tennenholtz, 2004): players hand a program to a trusted computer, programs can read each other, and "cooperate if you cooperate, otherwise defect" becomes a stable outcome of the one-shot game. Submitting that program is exactly choosing Commit. And the broader intuition — that a sanctioning institution flips a defection trap into cooperation — is the heart of the experimental commons literature, from Ostrom, Walker & Gardner's "Covenants with and without a sword" (1992) to Fehr & Gächter's work on how adding a punishment option turns defectors into cooperators. The quantum twist just relocates the enforcement from a human institution into physics.

Which leaves the genuinely hard problem exactly where politics always leaves it. The cooperative outcome needs a referee the players trust to supply the bracket. In the lab, the physicist builds it and everyone believes the apparatus. In society, *who builds and controls the referee* is the whole question — and physics offers no shortcut there.

I did wonder whether the same trick rescues the tragedy of the commons — the many-player version I explored in [an earlier post](/blog/14). It's tempting, but honesty compels a warning: that problem is substantially harder. With many players the quantum version stops looking like a clean two-line gate and starts looking like *N interacting spins*, and the elegant result here does not carry over unchanged. That's a story for another day — and one I'm still working out.
