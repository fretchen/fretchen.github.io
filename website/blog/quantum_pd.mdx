Over the last year, I really enjoyed diving a bit deeper into the prisoner's dilemma.  Interestingly, I recently understood that its quantum version provides a beautiful connection to the role of institutions, which a central pillar in modern political economics. 
What follows will be my attempt to explain the connection from the economics side because it seems possibly more consequential than the physics perspective.

## The basics of the prisoner's dilemma

I already wrote a full post on the [prisoner's dilemma](/blog/13) with Walter White and Jesse Pinkman, so is a quick summary wich the essential information that is important to this post. Jesse and Walter have been arrested and are interrogated separately, each offered the same deal: betray your partner and testify for immunity, or stay loyal and say nothing.

The sentences follow from both choices:

- Both stay loyal: 3 years each. 
- Both betray: 5 years each. 
- But if one betrays while the other stays loyal, the betrayer walks free while the loyal partner takes 15 years.

That last outcome is the crux of the dilemma. Whatever your partner does, betraying looks better — so both betray, and both end up worse off than if they had trusted each other. 

This leaves the question: what would it take to make loyalty the rational choice? 

## What an institution could look like here

The economics literature keeps returning to the same answer: an **institution** the players route their decisions through instead of acting in isolation. It does two things, and both are essential:

- First, it couples their choices: rather than chatting freely, each player submits a single move, and the mediator links the two by a rule fixed in advance. 
- Second, it enforces that rule — whatever it dictates becomes the sentence, with no appeal. 


In our version of the prisoners dilemma we can give this institution a face: **Saul Goodman**, the dirty lawyer both Walter and Jesse hire — and no neutral party: a client who testifies brings down the whole operation, Saul included. So he has every reason to make loyalty each man's own best move. And here is how he gets Jesse and Walter to cooperate. Being the thorough lawyer he is, Saul drafts *two* sworn statements for each client well before the interrogations: one that denies everything, and one that blames the other man. Each client's statements sit in the defense file as a small stack, and only the statement on top gets filed with the court — to start, both stacks have the denial on top. Each client then privately leaves Saul one standing instruction for what to do with the stacks. Saul carries the instructions out, files whatever ends up on top, and the court hands down the sentences by the original deal. The two men never speak; everything routes through Saul.

An instruction just tells Saul what to do with the stacks:

- **Stay loyal** — leave my stack alone; the denial gets filed.
- **Betray** — flip my stack: my testimony against him goes on top, and I take the immunity.
- **Flip both** — flip both stacks at once, his and mine together.

The first two instructions are just the original dilemma in new paperwork. The third — **Flip both** — is Saul's own invention: it chains your statement to your partner's, so the two stacks can only move together.  
That "flip both" instruction is the whole trick, and it is worth playing with. The logic of **Flip both** is worth tracing. If you flip both and your partner betrays, your instruction undoes their flip and lands on your own stack instead — the filed statements show *you* testifying and *them* denying everything, so you take the immunity and walk while they take the 15 years. If you both flip both, every stack is flipped twice and the file stays at mutual denial: three years each.

Now look at what happened to the trap. Against a partner who flips both, betraying is punished automatically — you jump straight to 15 years. So neither player wants to deviate from *both flipping both*: that gives each of them just 3 years, while going loyal drops you to the 5-year mutual-betrayal outcome and betraying costs you 15. Mutual loyalty has become the stable, self-interested choice.

## Making the connection to the quantum game

At this stage you can make a connection that still strikes me as fairly surprising.
The exact game we just described is also known, in a completely different language, to the quantum-physics community as the [Eisert–Wilkens–Lewenstein](https://journals.aps.org/prl/abstract/10.1103/PhysRevLett.83.3077) prisoners dilemma.

In this case, the picture above is just a **quantum circuit**. The two lines are two qubits. The mediator's bracket is the entangling operations $\hat{J}$ (before) and $\hat{J}^\dagger$ (after). The three moves are single-qubit gates: staying loyal is doing nothing, Betray is a bit-flip of your own qubit — and **Flip both, remarkably, is a phase-flip of your own qubit alone** — in quantum language it is named $\hat{Q}$: entanglement carries the local action onto both.
[Van Enk & Pike, *Physical Review A*, 2002](https://journals.aps.org/pra/abstract/10.1103/PhysRevA.66.024306) showed that this discrete quantum game is *mathematically identical* to the classical mediated game we built with Saul and his case file. The entanglement isn't doing anything a trusted enforcer couldn't do. In the quantum version, the laws of physics play the role of the enforcer: unitarity is the sanction, and it executes the punishment automatically, simultaneously, with no communication and no one to bribe.

## Connecting the dots and outlook

The whole approach, really seems to have strong parallels to what economists call a **commitment device** 
— the general idea that letting people lock in a rule ahead of time, one enforced automatically, can turn a defection trap into cooperation. 
The quantum twist just seems like a different mathematical framework to set up the communication channels.

This made me wonder whether the same trick rescues the tragedy of the commons — the many-player version I explored in [an earlier post](/blog/14). It's tempting, but mathematically not trivial. Each player has multiple options, which means that you better treat them as spins of length larger than 1/2, which are interacting with each other. No idea if the elegant result here can be carried over, I certainly did not find any literature on this until now. However, I would be super curious to learn
if anyone has gone done this road before and showed if the quantum language has indeed the potential that I am speculating about. Or not.
