Over the last year, I really enjoyed diving a bit deeper into the prisoner's dilemma.  Interestingly, I recently understood that its quantum version provides a beautiful connection to the role of institutions, which a central pillar in modern political economics. 
What follows will be my attempt to explain the connection from the economics side because it seems far more interesting than the physics perspective.

## The basics of the prisoner's dilemma

I recently, already wrote a blog post on the [prisoner's dilemma](blog/13/). The two players, Jesse and Walter, are in the following situation:


This is the **perfect** Prisoner's Dilemma scenario. Walter and Jesse each have exactly two choices:

- **Cooperate (C)**: Stay loyal to your partner — "I don't know this person, we've never met."
- **Defect (D)**: Blame your partner — "He forced me into this, he's the real criminal."

The sentences depend on what both choose:

- **Both cooperate (C, C)**: Each gets 3 years. The DEA has some evidence but not enough for major charges without testimony.
- **One blames the other (D, C)**: The betrayer walks free with immunity, the cooperator gets 15 years for "being the mastermind."
- **Both blame each other (D, D)**: Each gets 5 years. Their contradictory stories help neither case.

The prisoner's dilemma gives a beautiful insight into the limits of cooperation: it shows that the intuitively sensible move — cooperating — is almost never the "rational" one. Two people would each be better off if both cooperated, yet each, reasoning individually, ends up defecting. I wrote about this at length in [an earlier post](/blog/13), so here I'll keep it to the essentials.

Two players. Each can **cooperate** or **defect**. If both cooperate, both do reasonably well. If both defect, both do badly. But if one defects while the other cooperates, the defector grabs the biggest prize and the cooperator is left with nothing. Whatever the other player does, defecting looks better — so both defect, and both end up worse off than if they had trusted each other.

This obviously leaves you wondering what it would take to make cooperation rational. Not a lecture on morality — an actual change to the game that makes trusting each other the sensible choice.

## What an institution could look like here

If you look into the literature, the same answer keeps coming back: some kind of *institution*, an arbiter that changes the players' incentives. And it doesn't have to be an advisor whispering suggestions. The interesting case is an arbiter with **teeth** — one that can enforce outcomes, not merely recommend them. Think of Hobbes's Leviathan, or a regulator that fishers actually have to obey.

Here is what such an institution can look like concretely. Introduce a trusted referee. Each player secretly hands the referee a move; the referee looks the pair up in an agreed-upon table and pays out. The players never talk to each other — all the coordination flows through the institution.

Crucially, the referee offers a **third option** beyond cooperate and defect. Call it **Commit**. The best way to understand the three moves is as instructions written on a shared scorecard that starts at "both cooperate":

- **Cooperate** — leave the scorecard alone.
- **Defect** — flip *my own* letter (grab the temptation prize by switching myself from cooperator to defector).
- **Commit** — flip *both* letters at once, tying the two ledgers together.

That "flip both" move is the whole trick, and it is worth playing with. Play with it for a moment and the logic of **Commit** appears. If you Commit and the other player Defects, your "flip both" cancels their "flip mine" and lands the flip on *their* letter instead — the scorecard reads that *they* defected and *you* didn't, so you take the big prize and they get nothing. If you both Commit, the two "flip both" moves cancel entirely and the scorecard stays at mutual cooperation. Here is the full table the referee enforces (your payoff first, theirs second):

|  | They Cooperate | They Defect | They Commit |
|---|---|---|---|
| **You Cooperate** | 3, 3 | 0, 5 | 1, 1 |
| **You Defect** | 5, 0 | 1, 1 | 0, 5 |
| **You Commit** | 1, 1 | 5, 0 | 3, 3 |

Now look at what happened to the trap. Against a committer, defecting is punished automatically — you drop to the sucker's payoff of zero. So neither player wants to deviate from *both committing*: that gives each of them 3, and any unilateral change makes them worse off. Mutual cooperation has become the stable, self-interested choice. The institution didn't preach; it just made the punishment for defection credible, and built it into the rules.

## Making the connection to the quantum game

At this stage you can make a connection that still strikes me as fairly deep. The exact game we just described — the two lines, the referee's bracket, the "flip both" move — is also known, in a completely different language, to the quantum-physics community.

That picture above is a **quantum circuit**. The two lines are two qubits. The referee's bracket is the entangling operation physicists call J (before) and J† (after). The three moves are single-qubit gates: Cooperate is doing nothing, Defect is a bit-flip, and **Commit is a phase-flip** — the move physicists write as Q̂. Run the quantum mechanics and you get exactly the payoff table above. This is the celebrated Eisert–Wilkens–Lewenstein construction, and its headline result is precisely the one we found by hand: entanglement lets the players escape the prisoner's dilemma.

What I find striking is how thoroughly the mystery dissolves once you notice the equivalence. There is a known result — van Enk & Pike, *Physical Review A*, 2002 — showing that this discrete quantum game is *mathematically identical* to the classical mediated game we built with a scorecard and a referee. The entanglement isn't doing anything a trusted enforcer couldn't do. In the quantum version, the laws of physics play the role of the Leviathan: unitarity is the sanction, and it executes the punishment automatically, simultaneously, with no communication and no one to bribe.

## Connecting the dots and outlook

Once you see the referee for what it is, the quantum game stops being exotic and starts rhyming with a lot of economics.

The "Commit" move is essentially a **commitment device**. In mechanism design there is a clean version of this called *program equilibrium* (Tennenholtz, 2004): players hand a program to a trusted computer, programs can read each other, and "cooperate if you cooperate, otherwise defect" becomes a stable outcome of the one-shot game. Submitting that program is exactly choosing Commit. And the broader intuition — that a sanctioning institution flips a defection trap into cooperation — is the heart of the experimental commons literature, from Ostrom, Walker & Gardner's "Covenants with and without a sword" (1992) to Fehr & Gächter's work on how adding a punishment option turns defectors into cooperators. The quantum twist just relocates the enforcement from a human institution into physics.

Which leaves the genuinely hard problem exactly where politics always leaves it. The cooperative outcome needs a referee the players trust to supply the bracket. In the lab, the physicist builds it and everyone believes the apparatus. In society, *who builds and controls the referee* is the whole question — and physics offers no shortcut there.

I did wonder whether the same trick rescues the tragedy of the commons — the many-player version I explored in [an earlier post](/blog/14). It's tempting, but honesty compels a warning: that problem is substantially harder. With many players the quantum version stops looking like a clean two-line gate and starts looking like *N interacting spins*, and the elegant result here does not carry over unchanged. That's a story for another day — and one I'm still working out.
