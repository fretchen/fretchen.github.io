# Critique: Ostdeutsche Spediteure stehen unter Kostendruck — aber wovon eigentlich?

**Target audience:** East Germans, 30–60, who know fuel prices and Polish competition firsthand, are skeptical of the AfD but lack clear counter-arguments — they want honest engagement with a real problem, not a lecture.

**Plan file:** Found at `website/blog/spediteur_national.plan.md`. The post deliberately departs from the plan's narrative format (characters, dialogue, widgets) in favour of a data essay. This is a valid authorial choice; plan divergence is not flagged as an issue here.

**Overall impression:** The post has improved substantially — the Maut finding is genuinely surprising and the conclusion reframes the question cleanly. Two things still undermine it: a cluster of small but credibility-damaging typos and grammatical errors in §1, and a logical gap around the Mindestlohn column that the target audience will notice immediately. Both are fixable in one pass.

---

## Critical Issues

- [ ] **[§ Was sind die Kosten]** Typo "sind alsetwa 31 % geringer sind als" — double *sind*, and "alsetwa" is a run-together word (should be "als etwa"). An East German reader who is already a bit sceptical of urban know-it-alls will clock this and question the author's care. Fix before publishing.

- [ ] **[§ Was sind die Kosten]** Grammatical error: "gilt die die Maut" — double *die*. Same credibility risk as the typo above; both errors cluster in the same paragraph, which compounds the effect.

- [ ] **[§ Was sind die Kosten]** The Mindestlohn column exists in the table (7% gap if law is followed vs. 12% in practice) but the gap between the two scenarios is never explained. The target audience will immediately ask: "Why don't Polish drivers just get the German minimum wage — isn't that already the law?" Leaving this unanswered makes the table feel incomplete and plants a doubt that distracts from the Maut finding.

---

## Suggestions

- [ ] **[§ Was sind die Kosten]** The first paragraph ("Das Speditionsgeschäft hat enormen Kostendruck, Margen sind dünn") tells the target audience something they already know. It delays reaching the data. Cutting it entirely would make the section opener stronger.

- [ ] **[§ Was sind die Kosten / Intro]** Two consecutive "Das [verb] mich" transitions — "Das motivierte mich" (intro) followed immediately by "Das brachte mich dazu" (§1 opener) — feel mechanical. The section opener could begin directly with what was done rather than restating the motivation.

- [ ] **[§ Eine Interpretation]** "SES" in the table header ("Fahrer (Ost-DE, SES)") is unexplained. The target audience has no idea what SES stands for. Right at the moment the post is building toward its key finding, this creates a stumble. Either expand to "Fahrer (Ost-DE, Durchschnittslohn)" or add a one-line footnote.

- [ ] **[§ Eine Interpretation]** The ratio "2,7:1 → 2,3:1" is precise but abstract for this audience. A plain-language parallel — e.g., "polnische Fahrer verdienten 2018 gut ein Drittel so viel wie ostdeutsche, heute fast die Hälfte" — would land the point more concretely.

- [ ] **[§ Appendix]** The Tschechische Maut source is listed in the sources table but Tschechien appears nowhere in the main text or narrative tables. An attentive reader will wonder what was cut. Either remove the row or add a one-sentence explanation.

---

## Nitpicks

- [ ] **[§ Frontmatter]** `category: "other"` should be `category: "others"` — all other non-blockchain posts use `"others"`.

- [ ] **[§ Frontmatter/Description]** Double space in "Ostdeutsche  Spediteure" in the description.

- [ ] **[§ Intro]** "Jemandem" — capitalised mid-sentence; should be "jemandem".

- [ ] **[§ Was sind die Kosten]** "deutschen Mindestlohns (Mindestlohn)" — the parenthetical repeats the same word already used in German. The parenthesis adds nothing and can be deleted.

- [ ] **[§ Eine Interpretation]** The final sentence introduces "Grenzschutz" and "Marktzugangsschranken" without setup — these political terms appear only here, at the very end, without having been introduced in the intro. The conclusion would feel more earned if the framing ("Grenzschutz as the alternative") were at least briefly anchored to the intro's question.
