# Critique: Focused cybersecurity for a small project

**Target audience:** Technically literate builders of small projects who are NOT security specialists (indie devs, solo maintainers, small-team engineers). Comfortable with "dependency", "API", "private key", "endpoint"; have Dependabot/CodeQL on but treat security as an anxiety checklist. Secondary: blockchain/web3 builders who get bonus value from the on-chain specifics.

**Plan file:** Found. Target Audience section is up to date, but the rest of the plan (Outline, Core Thesis wording, Tone) is now stale — it still describes a "sprint that triggered this post" intro and a "The model also tells you when to stop" section, both of which the post no longer has. The shipped post diverges from the plan's outline (see Suggestions).

**Overall impression:** The spine is strong and genuinely useful — the house analogy, the plain-language asset categories, the verdict table, and the 87→1 Dependabot funnel all land well for the target audience. The two biggest problems are (1) a scatter of typos, several in the most prominent positions (paragraph one, and the core term "threat model" itself), which will cost trust with an audience that is judging whether this author knows what they're doing; and (2) an audience-consistency wobble — the asset side is beautifully de-jargoned, but the actor table and the "how would they get in" section snap back to dense on-chain vocabulary that the primary reader was just spared.

## Critical Issues

- [ ] **[§ Introduction]** Two errors in the very first paragraph: "whatever security feature could I find" (word order) and "what I am are protecting against" (doubled verb "am are"). For a post whose whole pitch is *rigor and focus*, typos in the opening two sentences undercut the author's credibility before the argument even starts. This is the highest-leverage fix.
- [ ] **[§ How would they actually get in?]** Line "this is really where the thread models starts to pay off" mistypes the post's central concept — it should be "threat model". Getting the core term wrong (and the number agreement, "models starts") is the kind of error the target audience notices and remembers. Fix with priority.
- [ ] **[§ Getting started with the asset list]** The closing lines "the whole decentral architecture really simplifies the / the security work massively too me" contain a doubled word ("the the" across the line break), "too me" (should be "to me"), and "decentral" (should be "decentralized"). Three errors in the section's punchline sentence blunt an otherwise strong point (you can't lose what you don't hold).

## Suggestions

- [ ] **[§ Who would like to steal these assets?]** The actor table is the sharpest audience-mismatch in the post. Right after you simplified assets into plain categories, this table reintroduces "reads deployed ABIs", "crafted ERC721 receivers", "authorizedProviders", "cross-origin requests", "reentrancy". The primary reader (non-security, possibly non-blockchain) hits a wall here. Consider a plain-language "Primary access" column, or a one-line lead-in that tells the general reader they can skim the technical cells.
- [ ] **[§ How would they actually get in?]** This is the densest section for the primary audience (SC01/SC05/API4/API5, CORS, EIP-3009, CollectorNFT, `/settle`). It serves the secondary blockchain reader well, but the primary reader has no handrail. Consider opening with one plain sentence on what "map surfaces to a known catalog" buys you, and/or moving the per-item specifics into a `<details>` block so the narrative stays accessible.
- [ ] **[§ Grading … four commits]** The heading and lead say "four commits" but the table lists **five** rows. The target audience will count the rows. Either reconcile the number or explain that one commit bundled two fixes.
- [ ] **[Frontmatter — description]** The description still says "Here's what they revealed after a week of security work." The "week of security work / sprint" framing was cut from the post — the opening is now about vague unease, not a time-boxed sprint. Description and post no longer match; a reader arriving from a link/preview gets a different promise than the intro delivers.
- [ ] **[§ Introduction vs body]** The intro's fourth question — "did I actually do a good enough job — or am I just buying more locks?" — is the most memorable line in the setup, but the body now answers it only tersely in the "Did you do enough?" bullet. The vivid payoff (deciding *against* a heavier lock) was cut. Consider restoring one concrete "I chose not to do X because my attackers can't reach it" beat so the fourth question pays off as strongly as the first three.
- [ ] **[§ Who would like to steal these assets?]** The sentence "But is it realistic that such attacks come from for a project the barely generates any mint fees?" has several grammar slips ("come from for", "the barely generates"). The point is good; the phrasing will read as unpolished to the audience.
- [ ] **[§ Cutting through the Dependabot noise]** The "one relevant dependency" is left deliberately vague ("a dependency deep in the serverless code"). For a section that otherwise wins on concreteness (87, 41, 2, exactly 1), naming the actual package — even in half a sentence — would make the funnel land harder. Optional, since vagueness was a deliberate choice, but worth reconsidering now that the section is otherwise so specific.

- [ ] **[§ Who would like to steal these assets?]** Heading has a double space before the "?": "these assets  ?".
- [ ] **[§ How would they actually get in?]** The two payoff bullets ("grading my own fixes…", "triaging an incoming CVE…") start lowercase; the post's other bullet lists start capitalized. Minor inconsistency.
- [ ] **[§ Grading … four commits]** The heading "Application example: Grading the usefulness of four commits against the model" is long and slightly clunky next to its sibling "Application example: Cutting through the Dependabot noise". Consider tightening for parallelism.
- [ ] **[Whole post]** Several paragraphs contain hard mid-sentence line breaks (intro, the two asset-section paragraphs). They render harmlessly in MDX (newlines become spaces), but the wrapping is inconsistent with other paragraphs — worth normalizing for maintainability.
- [ ] **[§ Cutting through the Dependabot noise / § Grading]** Two adjacent sections both hinge on "exactly one" (one real bug; exactly one alert). The parallel is fine, but back-to-back it risks feeling formulaic — worth a glance to ensure the phrasing differs.
- [ ] **[Double blank lines]** Extra blank lines between sections (after the actor section, before "How to build one") — harmless, but tidy up for consistency.
